About

Details

My biggest interests are in the areas of Distributed Computing and Machine Learning; the latter of which is backed up with graduate-level coursework in neural networks. I see them both playing key roles in the acceleration of scientific discovery and optimization of currently inefficient processes, in addition to just being fascinating in theory.

My work shows a general trend in systems programming and Deep Learning, but I think I can make the most strides in quickening their adoption by novices and professionals alike. As an introvert raised to be an extrovert, a backend heavy programmer interested by UI/UX, I straddle lines. I'd like to one day use my emerging leadership capabilities and knack for design to make these enigmatic yet powerful technologies more accessible to those who could use them most.

Why Computer Science?

I once heard a saying that folks go into the computing field for two main reasons: A) Comfort, and/or B), Impact. While it's up to the individual to decide which is more important, my journey to computer science has been a curved one pulled by the latter -- A longing to make this home of ours a little bit brighter, however indirect it may be.

So I tried my hand out. I was an agent for Search and Rescue in Seattle for many years, and had the privilege of leading non-profits. Although I strongly considered a career in medicine -- as I saw it to be the best way to serve others--, I also took a step back to consider a less traditional option: computing.

It soon dawned on me just how much influence a single person could have in technology. Instead of making diagnoses personally, I could help make a diagonstic tool that might aid countless doctors. What if instead of becoming an environmental engineer, I improved routing algorithms to save internet users time, and thus energy? Could I indirectly help labs the world over by increasing the speed and experience of scientific computation? It was over before it began. An interest in the What, How, and Why of this discipline with yet so much untapped potential took hold.

High Throughput Autonomous Proteomic Pipeline

Details

From June 5th to date, I've worked as a software developer for the Salomon Research Group, a Brown University Research Laboratory that looks into how we can better understand certain diseases via T-cell signaling. In particular, the exploration of what protein sites are phosphoryllated (read: activated) in certain cells at certain times, and how they fare with those changes. The gravity of breakthroughs in this field can't be understated: Greater understanding of proteins means a greater chance of solving threats such as a cancer and autoimmune disease, as well as the opportunity to repair or enhance our bodies at its most fundamental level.

Massive amounts of proteomic data are processed by the lab every day, so balancing speed and performance with our resources is a unique challenge. The simplified flow of computation is this: some number of files (often several GB) containing protein data come off the machines. We then take those and run it through an algorithm to match up our sequences of proteins against a known database, as well as perform extraction of other pertinent data. We then perform quantitation and filtering, and load our results into a relational database for our researchers to parse through.

For many years, our lab has been using a proprietary engine called MASCOT to take the protein files, match against the database, and give results. Yet the field has shifted. A new algorithm called Andromeda from the the Max Planck instute of Biochemistry supposedly gives more accurate results and a higher yield of data. My job was to investigate this code, and build an automated version of it that would fit into our already highly automated pipeline.

Implementation

I dove into our extensive Java code base, which was over a decade old and maintained by numerous past programmers, and definitely an exercise in of itself. I worked over the course of the summer to implement a fully automated pipeline for the laboratory that fit into the existing without altering the flow of control. I learned a great deal in the way of software engineering, scaling systems, XML parsing, file transferring, computational biology, and good old data scrubbing (to make sure that the results fit in nicely along the way). I also learned that specification is everything. At first I imagined the application having its own GUI for a researcher to create a parameter file. However, later iterations axed this concept of extensibility quickly. There could only be so many parameter files for the experiments we ran. Having a GUI to create them would be needlessly complicated, so I just pre-generated them and automatically pulled them from the database as needed. Practicality was king here, and I realized that a good software engineer needs to adapt to the needs of their users.

Results

Results were encouraging, to say the least. My automated version of the algorithm improved yield of critical data by 10-20%, but that number was sometimes as high as 40%. As my professor said: "It's like we hit oil!". That being said, this particular algorithm was slower than the old one. This is due in part to the fact that it generates much more data, but I've made it my goal to get the runtime as low as possible. I've since worked with multithreading in an attempt to try to speed things up, and even tried the hardware side to see the effect of Solid State Drives (faster reads/writes, which is particularly important here). Some approaches have worked, and some haven't, but that's just the name of the game. Optimization never stops.

Responsive Redesign of Hacker News

Details

The premise of this UI/UX design project was simple: Find a website outside of the 100 most popular, and try your hand at redesigning it (making sure that it's responsive along the way). No Wix, no bootstrap. Do your best with raw HTML/CSS. I thought about Hacker News, and how utilitarian it felt (probably by design). General design principle suggests one third pictures, one third text, and one third whitespace for a website. What if I tried it a more modern way? I thought that maybe users could benefit from a bit more visual stimuli. I conceived a top nav bar with a responsive, scrolling grid system, where each article has its own "box", complete with sampler text, image, title, and buttons. I figured that this could give users more info per unit, as well as being easier on the eyes.

As a disclaimer, I know that there are valid reasons behind sites like hackernews and reddit having long lists of links with minimal pictures. There are undoubtedly many fans who would have choice words for anybody trying to change them, but I thought it would be an interesting challenge.

Implementation

I sketched out five different web pages (home, user screen, comment screen, submit screen, and the jobs section), although I ended up redesigning the home screen. I made additions to each screen to decrease the severity of user errors, and generally make navigation a clearer experience.

Previous Next

In making a higher fidelity version of the homepage, the main change was the decision to move from a vertical list of links to a more grid-like representation of articles, using a thumbnail and the first few lines of the article (or discussion) as a summary to entice the reader on the right. This is much more visually appealing to a user (and in some cases easier to parse due to its balance of elements) than a dump of links surrounded by whitespace. Navigation has been improved somewhat by moving the search bar to a more appropriate position on the top, instead of the bottom. Finally, a darker theme was chosen to give more contrast than its beige predecessor (see below). We make a tradeoff in terms of the total links available per page, but I think it's worth the sacrifice

Bear in mind that it's not fully functional by any means, but more of a concept. Overall, I think it accomplishes what it sets out to do. Without the abstractive power of a framework like bootstrap, this gets the point across with just raw HTML/CSS, although it's not as polished given the limited time (~1 week). Despite gaining an appreciation for building a site from scratch, I think I can continue to improve on this with the cleanliness provided by an existing tool (ie. bootstrap).

Bash Command Line Implementation

Details

Disclaimer: I wrote this project for CS 0330, a class on computer systems/architecture. I have the code on a private repository as per my department's request for students showcasing projects. Leave me a message if you'd like to see it, and I'd be happy to share. All code and organization for this project is my own. No support functions were given.

The Bash CLI is a big deal for programmers. Simple, clean, and impressively powerful for managing our work. As a semester in Computer Systems began to ramp up, we were told to implement our own version of this shell that could replicate the functionality of the real one.

Implementation

Implementation over parts is key here. I began by diving into the most fundamental part: The REPL. After getting a working echo going, I started to parse the command line, ensuring that we're given valid input. I tokenized the buffer that I used to get user input into an array, and then checked it for file redirection. From there, I worked on handling builtin commands (cd, rm, ln, and exit), or otherwise passing off to a method that handled forking child processes and the call to execv (which is responsible for actually running the specified program). Basically, the flow of actual execution in the program.

But there was yet more to do. Testing all the way, I eventually had a working shell, but it couldn't handle signals or jobs very well yet. I then implemented a jobs list to handle multiple jobs (along with reaping zombie processes), signal handling to know when to quit, and moving jobs to the background or foreground. Although still not close to the real shell, it could function as one for most purposes. Overall, it was able to...

  • Handle error prone input at all levels
  • Run any specified programs and its arguments successfully
  • Handle builtin commands (cd, lm, rn, exit, fg, bg)
  • Keep track of multiple jobs (either stopped or in the background)
  • Reap jobs
  • Perform file redirection
  • Move jobs to foreground or background
  • Handle signals

Results

This project definitely honed both my systems and software engineering skillsets. Looking forward, it would be nice to be able to do relative pathing for commands or show what directory you're currently in. This was more of a quality of life improvement and thus wasn't as important as main functionality for the project, but still something cool to consider!

Startup Redesign: Guggy

Details

For this UI/UX project, we formed a group of four (Lisa Phinisee, Joshua Shao, Sophia Spitulnik) to recreate a Y Combinator product based solely off of their mission statement. We chose Guggy, a company that states...

Our specialized messaging-oriented NLP engine allows Guggy to turn any text message into a funny, personalized GIF!

We split up and brainstormed. What about a "photoshop" for recommended gifs? What about one that picked gifs for you based on your text message to splice into a movie? Concepts below.

Previous Next

Implementation

Our eventual design was a product that uses the details of a text message to search for related gifs, and then allows you to customize that gif. Caption, filter, change the speed!

You can do a walkthrough by clicking here.

The project culminated with more iteration: We presented our model to our peers, and received general feedback about how to clarify the user experience (adding captions to options, changing the export button) and how to improve the look and feel (changing color of navigation options, etc.). After getting even more clarifying advice from UserTesting.com, we felt that we were ready

Implementation

We can't really understate how much changed between iterations. Oftentimes it's too easy to just work on a project on our own, without consulting others. We work until we're proud, and then call it "done". In reality, is anything ever truly "done"? I'd argue not. Whether in design or code, it's important to keep iterating and showing progress, not results, at each stage.
